Building the Cable

   In 1856, the Atlantic Telegraph Company (ATC) was formed by Cyrus W. Field and English engineers John Watkins Brett and Charles Tilston Bright, both experts in submarine cables. The company's goal was to lay a cable across the Atlantic Ocean. At the start, they convinced the U.S. and Britain to subsidize the project. Even before the project started, there was much debate between revolutionary scientists about the design of the cable. Samuel Morse, inventor of the telegraph, and Michael Faraday, a pioneer in electromagnetism and chemistry, argued a narrow cable would result in a better signal. On the other hand, Lord Kelvin, the namesake of the scientific temperature unit, believed that a wider cable would improve the signal. The company sided with Faraday and Morse, since their proposal was much cheaper.

Tiffany's Transatlantic Cable Souvenir, 1858, Smithsonian Institution

   The cable was then manufactured with strands of copper and a thick layer of latex. The copper strands had a diameter of 0.083 inches, similar to the size of pencil lead. The final cable had a thickness of 0.625 inches, similar to the size of a No.2 pencil. The cable weighed 107 pounds per nautical mile. It had to be carried in two boats, which forced them to solve the logistics of connecting two cables in the ocean.

   On August 5, 1857, the company began laying the cable out. Due to a ship malfunction on the morning of August 3, the cable broke and was irretrievable. In the winter, they tried again, but the cable broke again due to a storm. During this time, many workers were quitting due to the pressure placed by governments and companies on this cable being built. Finally, with fortune on their side, in 1858 the cable was laid, and transatlantic communication became possible. 

   Throughout the endeavor, Britain played a pivotal role, with engineers like Brett and Bright lending their expertise to the project's success. As proponents of technological innovation, British scientists and engineers tirelessly worked alongside their American counterparts to overcome the myriad challenges posed by the ambitious undertaking. Moreover, the support garnered from Queen Victoria herself underscored the significance of the transatlantic cable not only for facilitating communication but also for strengthening diplomatic ties between nations.

   On August 16, 1858, Queen Victoria of England and U.S. President James Buchanan exchanged their first messages through the cable. At the time, it was estimated that it took one minute and 12 and a half seconds to send "Mississippi" across the cable. These slow speeds led some professors to attempt to come up with faster alternatives to Morse code.

   The completion of the cable caused a great celebration around the United States. Companies like Tiffany & Co. used leftover pieces of cable to make memorabilia. In Louisville, the laying of the cable was "the absorbing topic of conversation." In St. Louis, there was "great rejoicement." In Cincinnati, all business was suspended for a short time.

Historical Context
Turning Point

   From the British perspective, the laying of the transatlantic telegraph cable was seen as a monumental endeavor to connect the British Isles directly with its North American counterparts. The project garnered significant attention and support from the British government, recognizing the potential for improved communication and trade between the two continents.

   British engineers, such as John Watkins Brett and Charles Tilston Bright, played pivotal roles in the design and implementation of the cable, leveraging their expertise in submarine cables to overcome technical challenges and ensure the success of the project.

   Upon the successful completion of the cable, there was widespread celebration throughout Britain, with Queen Victoria herself acknowledging the achievement. The cable not only symbolized technological advancement but also strengthened the ties between Britain and its American allies, marking a significant milestone in transatlantic communication.
