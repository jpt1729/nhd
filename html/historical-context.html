Historical Context

In 1835, Samuel Morse, a professor at the University of New York, developed the first electromagnetic telegraph. This marked a turning point in communication as now cross-country communication could be achieved in minutes. The invention of the telegraph led to the improvement of old industries and creation of new industries. The telegraph allowed the press to report news at a pace never seen. New industries were formed to better use telegraphs, one of the most popular examples is the telephone. Still, the telegraph did not speed up transatlantic communication.

            The telegraph was a turning point in history in its own right. During the civil war, the US president, for the first time, could truly be the commander in chief getting information and sending orders in real time. On April 8, 1965, Abraham Linclion telegraphed the capture of the confederate capital Richmond, Virginia. Just a week later, the telegraph office announced his assassination to America.

“Abraham Lincoln died this morning at 22 minutes after Seven.”

Cyrus W. Field, an American who made his fortune on paper, envisioned a telegram cable linking New York and Newfoundland. He had heard of the many cables connecting England and France. Field then expanded his dream from connecting New York and Newfoundland to connecting both hemispheres.
